### Reflections on Autonomous Driving & Simulation

Attending the conference got me thinking about the significant push in the autonomous vehicle industry toward foundation models (VLAs) and generative models for simulation. Specifically, I hope there isn't an over-reliance on these models to bypass or fix fundamental issues that stem from traditional approaches. 

Take, for example, the problem of fog in LiDAR sensing. It is well-known that LiDAR struggles in these conditions; an end-to-end model trained to act even during these long-tail cases might end up performing uninformed, hazardous maneuvers. Solving this problem at the sensor or physics level—using a more traditional, deterministic manner—might be a much safer approach than trying to simply "train our way out of it."

On the generative simulation side, failing to model sensor physics faithfully will inevitably widen the sim-to-real gap, leading to poor performance in real-world deployments. That said, a great takeaway from the tutorial given by Waabi is that high-fidelity simulation remains absolutely key. We simply cannot rely on dangerous, long-tail edge cases naturally appearing in real-world data collection fleets. We must prepare for these edge cases in simulation before they ever manifest on the road. 

While I am sure the industry is well aware of these limitations, I still caution against over-trusting these models based solely on their incredible performance in nominal, ideal conditions. Moving forward, I would love to see benchmarking metrics shift to focus specifically on performance in challenging, safety-critical scenarios.
